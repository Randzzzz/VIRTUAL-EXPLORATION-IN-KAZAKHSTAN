Kazakhstan Geography

Kazakhstan, the world’s largest landlocked country and the ninth-largest overall, spans approximately 2,724,900 square kilometers. Located in Central Asia, with a small portion extending into Eastern Europe, Kazakhstan shares borders with Russia to the north, China to the east, and Kyrgyzstan, Uzbekistan, and Turkmenistan to the south. To the west, the country meets the Caspian Sea, the world’s largest inland body of water. This vast expanse of land showcases an incredible diversity of landscapes, ranging from sweeping steppes and deserts to towering mountains and large lakes.

The country experiences a continental climate, characterized by harsh winters and hot summers, with temperatures ranging from −40°C in the north to 40°C in the south. Rainfall is generally low, making much of Kazakhstan dry and semi-arid. Despite this, the fertile northern steppes support large-scale wheat production, and the country is rich in oil, gas, coal, and uranium, making it an economic powerhouse in Central Asia. Positioned strategically between Europe and Asia, Kazakhstan plays a vital role in global trade, particularly along the modern Silk Road.

Kazakhstan, boasts a vast and diverse landscape shaped by its continental climate and geographical features. Its terrain is dominated by a mix of deserts, semi-deserts, steppes, and forests, each contributing to the country's unique natural beauty. Deserts cover 26% of the land, while semi-deserts extend over 44%, followed by 6% forested areas and 24% steppe terrain. In addition to these primary landscapes, Kazakhstan also features mountains, rivers, and lakes that add to its rich ecological diversity.
Kazakhstan Language

Kazakh as the state language of Kazakhstan and Russian as an official language used in government and business. Kazakh, a Turkic language, is spoken by approximately 80% of the population and is written in the Cyrillic script, though the government has planned a transition to the Latin alphabet by 2025 to modernize and integrate with global communication systems. Russian, spoken by over 83% of the population, remains widely used in administration, education, and daily interactions, especially in urban areas.

Besides Kazakh and Russian, Kazakhstan is home to a linguistically diverse population, including speakers of Uzbek, Ukrainian, Uyghur, Tatar, and German due to the country's rich ethnic mix. In recent years, English has gained popularity, particularly among the younger generation and professionals, as Kazakhstan strengthens its global connections. The government promotes trilingual education (Kazakh, Russian, and English) to enhance international opportunities for its citizens.

Five Must Go Places
Nur-Sultan

The capital city is known for its modern architecture, including the iconic Baiterek Tower, the Palace of Peace and Reconciliation, and the Khan Shatyr shopping center, which is shaped like a massive tent. The city blends futuristic design with Kazakh culture, making it a must-visit destination.

Charyn Canyon

Its deep gorges and towering cliffs make it a paradise for hikers, photographers, and adventure seekers. The famous Valley of Castles is one of the most breathtaking areas.

Lake Kaindy

This mystical lake was formed after an earthquake triggered a landslide, creating a submerged forest with tall trees sticking out of the turquoise waters. It’s a surreal and picturesque destination, perfect for kayaking, hiking, and nature lovers.

Altyn-Emel National Park

This vast national park is home to the famous Singing Dune, a massive sand dune that produces a musical sound when the wind blows. The park also features unique rock formations, canyons, and diverse wildlife, including wild horses and gazelles.

Almaty

Kazakhstan’s former capital, is the country’s largest city and cultural heart. Visitors can explore Panfilov Park, Zenkov Cathedral, and the Central State Museum. The city is also surrounded by stunning Tien Shan Mountains, offering easy access to Shymbulak Ski Resort and Big Almaty Lake.
